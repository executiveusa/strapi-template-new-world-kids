Executive summary

The homepage looks finished. It is not functional.

Pass 3 — requested by the client directly: "the color scheme is tacky and doesn't look prime." All four Pass 1 functional P0s are now fixed or reclassified (see below), so this pass evaluates the site with working navigation, working donation, and a real light theme in place. The verdict on the client's specific complaint: the color scheme critique is correct. It is not a taste-only nitpick — it's evidence-backed by real screenshots (below) and it caps the score.

The problem is not the presence of green — the site is a garden/agriculture nonprofit, so green photography is correct. The problem is that the UI chrome stacks green on top of green photography on top of a brassy "harvest gold" accent used for every eyebrow label, producing a flat, monochrome, low-contrast wall of forest tones with almost no relief. Combined with tight card padding, thin near-invisible borders (1px at 10% opacity), and minimal variation in section pacing, the result reads heavier and more dated than the quality of the copy and photography underneath it deserves.

Microinteractions are present (Framer Motion whileInView reveals, button hover states) but subtle to the point of being invisible in normal use, and there is no single signature moment — nothing that would read as deliberately, distinctively "this site" versus a competent template. That is the gap between where the site is now (7.1, usable but not client-ready) and an awwwards-tier bar (9+).

Recommended direction below (not yet implemented — audit only, per phase gating): let the photography carry the greens, and shift the UI shell to a warmer, quieter neutral palette (ink/charcoal instead of forest-black, sand/stone instead of cream, terracotta reserved for the one CTA color, gold reserved for numbers only). Add real elevation and section-to-section pacing variation. Add a couple of signature microinteractions instead of many small identical fades.

Second pass — the actual book

Don't Make Me Think, Revisited: what changes with the primary source

The first pass used general Krug-informed principles. This pass reads the full text of the 2014 revised edition directly and checks it against the same findings — citing Krug's actual arguments and wording instead of paraphrasing them, and flagging anywhere the book surfaces something the first pass missed.

Persistent navigation — the required checklist SHARPENED / NEW

Krug names four elements persistent navigation must supply on every page: Site ID, Sections, Utilities (capped at four or five — the rest belong in the footer), and a way to search. His stated reason: navigation has three jobs — it reveals what's here, teaches how to use the site, and builds confidence in whoever built it.

“Navigation isn't just a feature of a Web site; it is the Web site … Without it, there's no there there.”

Sharpens P0-2 — the fix (real Site ID + Sections + Utilities in one header) now maps directly to Krug's own checklist, not a paraphrase of it.

The Trunk Test — verbatim

Confirmed present in this edition, six items: dropped on any page, blindfolded, you should be able to answer — What site is this? What page am I on? What are the major sections? What are my options here? Where am I in the scheme of things? How can I search?

Before this fix, NWKids failed this test on every page except the homepage's two-tab switcher. It's now the report's lead framework rather than a general Krug paraphrase.

Homepage-specific requirements SHARPENED / NEW

A homepage uniquely must answer, at a glance: What is this? Who runs it? What do they have here? What can I do here? Delivered via a tagline (6–8 words, a value proposition, not a motto) and a Welcome blurb — explicitly not a mission statement.

“Don't use a mission statement as a Welcome blurb … Nobody reads them.”

New angle: the pre-fix homepage had no tagline and no Site ID at all — it failed all four homepage-specific questions, not just the general trunk test.

Error pages / 404s SHARPENED / NEW

Not covered anywhere in the book — “404” and “error page” don't appear. The closer applicable material is the goodwill-reservoir concept below.

P2-3 (custom 404) has no direct Krug citation available — flagged honestly rather than inventing one.

The “reservoir of goodwill” and broken links SHARPENED / NEW

Every friction point drains trust; Krug says a single mistake can empty the reservoir entirely. Combined with his “scent of information” concept — links should give off a strong scent that clicking brings you closer to what you want — a dead link doesn't just add friction, it severs the scent trail completely.

“Know the main things that people want to do on your site and make them obvious and easy … If most people are coming to your site to apply for a mortgage, nothing should get in the way of making it dead easy to apply for a mortgage.”

New framing for P0-1: the broken donate button isn't one bug among many — it's the single mistake Krug specifically warns can empty the whole reservoir at once.

Mobile navigation — the specific failure Krug names SHARPENED / NEW

The mobile chapter doesn't discuss hamburger menus. The mistake Krug actually flags: tapping a deep link and landing on the mobile homepage instead of the content, forcing the visitor to hunt for what they came for.

“Don't leave me standing at the front door.”

Structurally identical to NWKids' problem — 7+ links (including the sticky mobile donate bar) dumping visitors on a 404 instead of content.

Pass 3 — requested directly by the client

Color, whitespace & the awwwards bar

Client feedback verbatim: the color scheme is "tacky," "doesn't look prime," and should feel like "natural nature" without being dominated by green. This pass captures fresh evidence against that specific complaint, scores it, and proposes a direction — no source files were edited to produce it.

Current palette (as shipped)

Read directly from apps/ui/src/app/globals.css. The issue isn't any single color — it's that three of these (forest, guayabo, gold) are all mid-saturation earth tones with similar lightness, so nothing in the UI chrome pulls forward. Gold in particular is doing double duty as both "premium accent" and "every single eyebrow label," which flattens its impact everywhere it's used.

Proposed direction (not implemented — for approval)

Core idea: let the photography and video carry the living green — that's correct and authentic for a garden nonprofit. Let the UI shell (backgrounds, cards, borders, nav, labels) shift to quieter, warmer neutrals so the green in the imagery has somewhere to stand out against. This is the same logic awwwards-tier sites use with hero photography: vivid image, quiet frame.

Rules that go with this palette: gold/ochre appears only on numerals (stats, prices) — not on eyebrow labels, which move to a quiet tracked-caps neutral. Sage green appears once, deliberately (one recurring UI element, e.g. active nav state or a single icon set), not smeared across backgrounds. Terracotta stays reserved for the single primary CTA color, exactly as coral is today. Dark mode moves from forest-black to true ink so it stops reading as "swampy" and reads as "quiet, premium dark."

Whitespace & pacing findings

  • Trust section card grid is dense: 6 cards, ~18px internal padding, 1px/10%-opacity borders that nearly disappear into the background — cards don't feel designed, they feel like bordered <div>s. Needs more internal padding, a visible border or soft shadow, and fewer cards per row on desktop (3 max, not 6-in-a-block).
  • No section-to-section pacing variation: every section from hero to footer uses the same background value and roughly the same vertical padding. Awwwards-tier sites vary section rhythm — alternating density, occasional full-bleed breathing room, a deliberately quiet section between two dense ones.
  • Donation tier cards are cramped: three cards, minimal gap, no elevation to separate the "Most impactful" card beyond a colored border — a shadow-based lift plus more internal padding would read as more considered.

Microinteraction & motion findings

  • What's there: consistent whileInView fade+slide entrances on nearly every section, button hover/tap states on primary CTAs, theme-toggle transition. All functionally correct and not distracting.
  • What's missing for an awwwards bar: every entrance uses the identical opacity+y-12px pattern — there's no signature moment. Recommend 2–3 deliberate, restrained additions: a magnetic/lift hover on the donate button (not just color change), a number count-up on the stats strip instead of static text, and a slightly staggered reveal on card grids instead of all cards fading in together. Per the motion laws in the brief's own spec, this should stay restrained — not parallax, not scroll-hijacking, just 2–3 moments that feel intentional.
